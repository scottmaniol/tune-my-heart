### Story 25
**Title:** The Kingdom Divides
**Scripture:** 1 Kings 12–13
**Key Verse:** "So Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day." — 1 Kings 12:19
**Simple Summary:** Rehoboam's harshness caused Israel to divide into two kingdoms, and Israel began worshiping idols. But God's plan to send Jesus, the perfect King, remained firm.
**Longer Story:**
After Solomon's death, his son Rehoboam ascended to the throne of Israel. However, the peace and prosperity that marked Solomon's reign were short-lived. Rehoboam's poor decisions quickly led to division and strife, fulfilling the consequences of Solomon's unfaithfulness to God.

When Rehoboam became king, the people of Israel came to him with a plea. They said, "Your father put a heavy burden on us. If you lighten the load, we will serve you." The request was clear: they wanted relief from the harsh policies of Solomon's reign, particularly the heavy taxation and forced labor that had been used to fund his building projects. Rehoboam, seeking wisdom, first consulted the elders who had served his father. They advised him, saying, "If you serve the people and answer them kindly, they will always be loyal to you."

But Rehoboam rejected the elders' advice and instead turned to his younger, less experienced friends. They encouraged him to take a more harsh approach, advising him to increase the burden on the people. Rehoboam heeded their counsel and responded to the people, saying, "My father made your yoke heavy; I will make it heavier. My father scourged you with whips; I will scourge you with scorpions." This response only served to anger the people, and as a result, ten of the twelve tribes of Israel rebelled against him. They made Jeroboam, one of Solomon's former officials, their king. Only the tribes of Judah and Benjamin remained loyal to Rehoboam, and thus, the kingdom of Israel was divided into two: the northern kingdom of Israel under Jeroboam and the southern kingdom of Judah under Rehoboam.

Jeroboam, fearing that the people might turn back to Rehoboam if they continued to worship in Jerusalem, took drastic measures. He set up golden calves in the cities of Bethel and Dan and declared to the people, "These are your gods who brought you out of Egypt." This act of idolatry led the northern kingdom into sin, distancing them from the true King of heaven.

The division of the kingdom served as a reminder that no earthly king could bring about God's perfect rule. Both the northern and southern kingdoms struggled with sin and rebellion, as their kings often led them astray.

However, God's promise to David remained intact. Despite the failures of earthly kings, God's covenant with David still held, and through David's line, the true King, Jesus, would one day come. Jesus would unite God's people and establish His eternal reign, fulfilling the promises made long ago. Through Him, God's perfect rule would be realized, not in a divided kingdom, but in a united, eternal kingdom.

**Talk About It:**
*   What caused the kingdom to divide after Solomon's death?
*   How did Jeroboam lead the northern kingdom into idolatry?
*   How does the divided kingdom remind us of the need for Jesus, the true King?

### Story 26
**Title:** God Raises Up Elijah
**Scripture:** 1 Kings 18
**Key Verse:** “Answer me, O Lord, answer me, that this people may know that you, O Lord, are God, and that you have turned their hearts back.” — 1 Kings 18:3
**Simple Summary:** Elijah showed Israel that God is the true King by calling down fire from heaven. God alone is worthy of worship.
**Longer Story:**
In the northern kingdom of Israel, many kings led the people further away from God, and the situation grew darker under the rule of King Ahab. Ahab, one of the most wicked kings, married Jezebel, a foreign queen who introduced the worship of Baal, a false god. Together, they built altars to Baal throughout the land, leading Israel further into idolatry. They also persecuted God’s prophets, killing many who spoke against their sin. But despite their evil, the true King of heaven did not abandon His people. He raised up a prophet named Elijah to call Israel back to worshiping Him.
Elijah was a bold and fearless prophet, and his first message to King Ahab was a declaration of God’s judgment: “As surely as the Lord lives, there will be no dew or rain in the land until I say so.” This drought was not just a natural disaster, but a sign that the gods Ahab worshiped were powerless. The land would suffer, but it would also serve as a reminder that God alone controls the heavens and the earth.
During the drought, God provided for Elijah in miraculous ways. He instructed Elijah to hide by a brook, where ravens brought him food each day. When the brook dried up, God sent Elijah to a widow in Zarephath, a place outside of Israel. Though the widow had only a handful of flour and a small jar of oil, God miraculously multiplied her supply, and she, her son, and Elijah were provided for throughout the famine. This showed that God was not only able to sustain Elijah, but He could provide even for those who trusted Him, even in the midst of scarcity.
After three years, God instructed Elijah to confront Ahab once more. He proposed a dramatic contest on Mount Carmel: “Gather the prophets of Baal, and I will call on the Lord. The God who answers by fire—He is God.” The prophets of Baal built an altar and called upon their god from morning till evening, shouting, dancing, and even cutting themselves, but no fire came. Then Elijah built an altar to the Lord, arranged the sacrifice, and drenched it with water, demonstrating the power of God’s intervention.
Elijah then prayed, “Lord, let it be known today that You are God in Israel.” Immediately, fire fell from heaven, consuming the sacrifice, the wood, the stones, and even the water around the altar. The people fell on their faces, crying, “The Lord—He is God!” Elijah commanded the people to seize the prophets of Baal, and after their destruction, rain poured down, ending the drought.
Elijah’s boldness pointed Israel back to the true King. Though many still rejected God, Elijah’s ministry reminded them that the King of heaven was the only one worthy of worship. Elijah’s story also points to Jesus, the ultimate prophet and King, who calls us to turn from sin and worship Him alone.
**Talk About It:**
*   How did Elijah show that Baal was a false god?
*   What happened when Elijah prayed on Mount Carmel?
*   How does Elijah’s ministry point us to Jesus, who calls us to worship the true King?

### Story 27
**Title:** Ahab’s Sin
**Scripture:** 1 Kings 21
**Key Verse:** “But there was no one like Ahab who sold himself to do wickedness in the sight of the Lord, because Jezebel his wife stirred him up.” — 1 Kings 21:25
**Simple Summary:** Ahab and Jezebel sinned greatly, but God saw their evil and judged them.
**Longer Story:**
In the days of King Ahab, there lived a man named Naboth who owned a beautiful vineyard in the city of Jezreel. Naboth’s vineyard was situated right next to the palace of King Ahab, and the king greatly desired it. He admired the vineyard’s lushness and thought it would make an excellent vegetable garden. So, King Ahab approached Naboth and offered to buy the vineyard, saying, “Let me have your vineyard to use for a vegetable garden. I’ll give you a better vineyard in exchange, or if you prefer, I’ll pay you its worth in money.”
But Naboth refused the king’s offer. He replied, “The Lord forbid that I should give you the inheritance of my ancestors.” The vineyard was not just a piece of land; it was part of Naboth’s family heritage, given to him by God. For him, it was a sacred trust, and he could not part with it.
King Ahab was greatly upset by Naboth’s refusal. He went home, laid down on his bed, and refused to eat. His frustration and disappointment were overwhelming. When his wife, Queen Jezebel, noticed his distress, she asked him what was wrong. Ahab explained that Naboth had refused to sell his vineyard and that it had made him so upset he could not even eat.
Jezebel, being a wicked and manipulative woman, reassured her husband, “You are the king of Israel!” she said. “Don’t let this trouble you. I’ll get you the vineyard you want.” Jezebel devised a devious plan to have Naboth falsely accused and executed. She wrote letters in Ahab’s name, sealed them with his royal seal, and sent them to the elders and nobles of Jezreel. In the letters, she instructed them to accuse Naboth of cursing God and the king, and to have him stoned to death.
The elders and nobles, who should have upheld justice, chose to follow Jezebel’s wicked plan. They gathered false witnesses to accuse Naboth, and he was sentenced to death. When Naboth was stoned to death, the news was sent to Jezebel, who then informed Ahab that Naboth was dead and that the vineyard was now his.
Ahab, eager to claim what he believed was rightfully his, went down to take possession of Naboth’s vineyard. But as soon as he entered the vineyard, the prophet Elijah was sent by God to confront him. Elijah confronted Ahab with these words: “Have you murdered and also taken possession? In the place where dogs licked Naboth’s blood, dogs will lick your blood.” Elijah delivered a message of judgment, declaring that Ahab’s house would be destroyed because of the wickedness he and Jezebel had done. The consequences of their actions were severe, and God’s justice would not be delayed.
This story serves as a powerful reminder of God’s justice and His intolerance for wickedness, especially when it is done in secret and for selfish gain.
**Talk About It:**
*   What did Ahab and Jezebel do to Naboth, and how did God respond?
*   How did Elijah deliver God’s judgment to Ahab and Jezebel?
*   What does this story teach us about God’s justice and the need for repentance?

### Story 28
**Title:** God Raises Up Elisha
**Scripture:** 2 Kings 5
**Key Verse:** “So he went down and dipped himself in the Jordan seven times, as the man of God told him, and his flesh was restored and became clean like that of a young boy.’” — 2 Kings 5:14
**Simple Summary:** Elisha performed miracles to show God’s power and care for His people.
**Longer Story:**
After Elijah’s dramatic ministry, God raised up Elisha to continue calling Israel back to Him. Elisha had been Elijah’s servant, faithfully following him and learning from his bold trust in God. When the time came for Elijah to be taken to heaven, Elisha made a bold request: “Let me inherit a double portion of your spirit.” This was a request for a special blessing, an acknowledgment of Elisha’s desire to continue the work of God that Elijah had started.
Elijah told Elisha that if he saw him being taken up to heaven, his request would be granted. As they walked together, a chariot of fire appeared and separated them. Elijah was taken to heaven in a whirlwind, and Elisha stood watching. This dramatic departure marked the end of Elijah’s earthly ministry, and Elisha’s ministry began. Elisha then picked up Elijah’s cloak, a symbol that God had chosen him to carry on Elijah’s work. It was a powerful moment that marked the beginning of Elisha’s prophetic calling.
Elisha’s ministry was marked by God’s power and compassion. He performed many miracles that showed the people that the true King of heaven was still with them. For example, Elisha healed a barren spring of water, making it safe to drink for the people of the city. He also multiplied a widow’s oil to save her family from debt, and he raised a boy from the dead, showing that God cared deeply for His people, both spiritually and physically.
One of Elisha’s most famous miracles involved a man named Naaman, a commander in the army of Aram. Naaman had leprosy, a terrible and debilitating disease. When he heard about Elisha, he traveled to Israel seeking healing. Instead of meeting with Naaman personally, Elisha sent a messenger with simple instructions: “Wash in the Jordan River seven times, and you will be healed.”
Naaman was initially angry. He expected a grand gesture, thinking that Elisha would call on the name of the Lord and perform a dramatic healing. “Aren’t the rivers of Damascus better than the waters of Israel?” he complained. But his servants urged him to obey the prophet’s instructions, and when he finally did, his skin was miraculously healed. Naaman returned to Elisha and declared, “Now I know there is no God in all the world except in Israel.”
Through these miracles, Elisha demonstrated that God is not only powerful but also deeply cares for His people. His ministry pointed both Israel and surrounding nations to the true King who rules with power and grace. Elisha’s story reminds us of Jesus, who performed even greater miracles, healing the sick, raising the dead, and offering forgiveness to sinners. Jesus fulfilled God’s promise to restore His creation and bring about the ultimate healing that only the true King can provide.
**Talk About It:**
*   How did Elisha receive a double portion of Elijah’s spirit?
*   What miracles did Elisha perform, and how did they show God’s care?
*   How does Elisha’s ministry remind us of Jesus’s miracles?

### Story 29
**Title:** Jonah Flees from God
**Scripture:** Jonah 1–2
**Key Verse:** “I called out to the Lord, out of my distress, and he answered me; out of the belly of Sheol I cried, and you heard my voice.” — Jonah 2:1-2
**Simple Summary:** Jonah tried to run from God, but God sent him to Nineveh to preach. The people repented, and God showed mercy, just as He does to us.
**Longer Story:**
God’s mercy is not just for Israel—it is for all nations. This truth became clear in the story of Jonah, a prophet called to deliver God’s message to Nineveh, the capital of Assyria. Nineveh was a wicked city, known for its cruelty and sin. The people there were enemies of Israel, and they had lived in rebellion against God. Yet, despite their evil ways, God, the King of heaven, wanted to offer them a chance to repent.
When God instructed Jonah to go to Nineveh, Jonah refused. He didn’t want the people of Nineveh to receive God’s mercy. Instead of obeying, Jonah boarded a ship bound for Tarshish, in the opposite direction. But Jonah could not escape the King’s command. God sent a powerful storm that threatened to destroy the ship. The sailors, terrified, cried out to their gods, but the storm did not cease. Finally, Jonah admitted that he was the cause of the storm, confessing, “I am running from the Lord, the God of heaven who made the sea and the dry land.” At Jonah’s urging, the sailors threw him overboard, and the sea immediately grew calm.
As Jonah sank into the depths of the sea, God sent a great fish to swallow him. Jonah spent three days and three nights in the belly of the fish, praying to God. In his prayer, Jonah acknowledged God’s sovereignty and said, “Salvation comes from the Lord.” Jonah recognized that, even in the darkest moments, God’s mercy was still available to him.
After three days, God commanded the fish to vomit Jonah onto dry land, giving him a second chance to obey. This time, Jonah went to Nineveh and proclaimed, “In forty days, Nineveh will be overthrown!” To Jonah’s astonishment, the people of Nineveh responded in repentance. From the king to the lowest citizens, they humbled themselves, fasted, and wore sackcloth as a sign of their sorrow for their sins. God saw their repentance and, in His mercy, spared the city from destruction.
However, Jonah was angry. He didn’t understand why God would show mercy to such a wicked people. “I knew You are a gracious and compassionate God,” Jonah complained. God gently corrected him, reminding Jonah that He cared for the people of Nineveh, and even the animals in the city. God’s compassion extends to all who repent, no matter how far they have gone astray.
Jonah’s story reminds us that the King’s mercy is available to all who repent, no matter their past. It points us to Jesus, who spent three days in the grave before rising again to bring salvation to the world. Just as Jonah was sent to proclaim mercy to Nineveh, Jesus came to offer mercy to all people who turn to Him.
**Talk About It:**
*   Why did Jonah run away from God’s command to go to Nineveh?
*   How did God show His power and mercy in Jonah’s story?
*   How does Jonah’s time in the fish remind us of Jesus’s death and resurrection?

### Story 30
**Title:** The Fall of Israel and Judah
**Scripture:** 2 Kings 17
**Key Verse:** “And the Lord removed Israel out of his sight, as he had said by all his servants the prophets. So Israel was carried away out of their own land to Assyria until this day.” — 2 Kings 17:23
**Simple Summary:** Israel and Judah turned away from God, and He sent them into exile.
**Longer Story:**
The northern kingdom of Israel had long turned away from God, with generation after generation of kings leading the people into idolatry. Worship of false gods like Baal, along with the establishment of golden calves, became widespread throughout Israel. Despite their persistent rebellion, the true King, God, never abandoned His people. He sent prophets like Elijah, Elisha, Amos, and Hosea to call the people back to Him. These prophets warned Israel about the consequences of their sin and urged them to repent and return to the Lord. But the people of Israel refused to listen, and their hearts grew harder.
One of the most poignant messages came from the prophet Hosea. Hosea’s plea to Israel was clear: “Return to the Lord, for He is gracious and compassionate.” Yet, Israel’s rebellion continued unabated, and God’s patience eventually wore thin.
The Assyrian Empire, a fierce and powerful nation, came against Israel, fulfilling the warnings of the prophets. The Assyrians captured Samaria, the capital of Israel, and the people were scattered into exile. The kingdom of Israel ceased to exist, and the Israelites were taken far from their homeland, scattered among the nations as a judgment for their sin. The people had refused to listen, and now the consequences of their disobedience had come to pass.
Meanwhile, the southern kingdom of Judah, although more faithful in some periods, followed a similar path. Judah had experienced more faithful kings, like Hezekiah and Josiah, who worked to restore proper worship of the true God. However, over time, even Judah turned away from God, following in Israel’s footsteps. Prophets such as Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel warned Judah that judgment was near unless they repented. Yet, the hearts of the people remained hard, and they continued in their idolatry.
Manasseh, one of the most wicked kings of Judah, led the people into even greater sin, building altars to false gods in the temple itself. Despite some efforts at reform, such as Josiah’s reign, the damage had been done. The people of Judah had hardened their hearts, and their fate was sealed.
God sent the Babylonians as the instrument of His judgment. King Nebuchadnezzar of Babylon besieged Jerusalem, and after a prolonged struggle, the city fell. The Babylonians burned the temple, destroyed the city walls, and took the people into exile, leaving Judah desolate. The land lay in ruins, a stark reminder of the consequences of turning away from the true King.
**Talk About It:**
*   Why did God send prophets like Hosea to Israel, and what was their message?
*   What happened to the northern kingdom of Israel when they refused to repent?
*   How does God’s promise of restoration point to Jesus?

### Story 31
**Title:** God Raises Up Jeremiah
**Scripture:** Jeremiah 1
**Key Verse:** “Then the Lord put out his hand and touched my mouth. And the Lord said to me, ‘Behold, I have put my words in your mouth.’” — Jeremiah 1:9
**Simple Summary:** God promised to be with Jeremiah and give him the words to speak. God showed Jeremiah that He would bring His plans to pass, and He would protect him.
**Longer Story:**
In a time when the people of Judah were turning away from God, God chose a young man named Jeremiah to be His prophet. Jeremiah was just a boy when God called him, and he felt unqualified for such a daunting task. When God spoke to him, He said, “Before I formed you in the womb, I knew you. Before you were born, I set you apart to be a prophet to the nations.” God had a special plan for Jeremiah even before his birth, giving him a unique purpose as a prophet to deliver God’s message to a nation in rebellion.
Despite this divine calling, Jeremiah was afraid. “I’m too young to speak for You, Lord,” he responded. But God assured him, saying, “Do not say you are too young. Wherever I send you, you will go, and whatever I command you, you will speak. Do not be afraid, for I am with you.” With these words, God equipped Jeremiah for the task ahead. God then touched Jeremiah’s mouth and declared, “I have put My words in your mouth. Today I appoint you over nations and kingdoms to uproot and tear down, to destroy and overthrow, to build and plant.”
God’s calling for Jeremiah was clear: he was to speak God’s words to the people, a message of judgment and hope. God’s words would bring both destruction and restoration. Jeremiah’s role was not only to speak judgment upon Judah’s sins but also to offer a future hope for the people. As God explained through visions, the almond tree symbolized the quick fulfillment of His word, just as the tree buds first in spring. Similarly, God’s word would soon bring swift consequences to Judah’s disobedience. The vision of the boiling pot tilting from the north symbolized the coming disaster from the Babylonians, who would bring destruction to Judah for its idolatry and sin.
Throughout his ministry, Jeremiah faced rejection, persecution, and sorrow, but he remained faithful. He delivered God’s message even though it was unpopular, warning the people of God’s impending judgment because of their rebellion. Yet, in the midst of his prophecies of judgment, God also provided hope. In a letter to the exiles, God spoke through Jeremiah, saying, “‘I know the plans I have for you,’ declares the Lord, ‘plans to prosper you and not to harm you, plans to give you a hope and a future.’” This promise, even in the face of judgment, reminded the people that God was not done with them.
Jeremiah’s story is a reminder that God’s words are powerful, and He speaks through those He calls, regardless of age or fear. Even in times of hardship and judgment, God’s plans remain good, and His purposes are for the hope and future of His people.
**Talk About It:**
*   What did God tell Jeremiah when he said he was too young to speak?
*   What do you think the almond tree and the boiling pot meant in Jeremiah’s visions?
*   How does God’s calling of Jeremiah remind us that God has a plan for each of us?

### Story 32
**Title:** We Will Not Bow
**Scripture:** Daniel 3
**Key Verse:** “Our God whom we serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace, and he will deliver us out of your hand, O king.” — Daniel 3:17
**Simple Summary:** Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused to bow to a false god. God saved them from the fiery furnace, showing He is the true King.
**Longer Story:**
During the Babylonian exile, when the Israelites were far from their homeland and living under foreign rule, their faith was put to the ultimate test. In this foreign land, surrounded by the worship of false gods and idolatry, three young men—Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego—stood firm in their devotion to the true King.
King Nebuchadnezzar built a massive golden statue, ninety feet tall, and commanded everyone in his kingdom to bow down and worship it. A decree was issued: anyone who refused would be thrown into a blazing furnace. When the music played, people from all nations bowed before the statue. But Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego did not bow. They served the King of heaven and would not worship any false god.
Their refusal was reported to Nebuchadnezzar, who summoned them in anger. “Is it true that you will not bow to my statue?” he demanded. “If you do not worship it, you will be thrown into the blazing furnace. Then what god will be able to rescue you from my hand?”
The three friends replied boldly, “O King, we do not need to defend ourselves. If we are thrown into the furnace, the God we serve is able to save us. But even if He does not, we want you to know that we will not bow to your gods or worship the image you have set up.”
Nebuchadnezzar’s anger burned hotter than the furnace itself. He ordered the fire heated seven times hotter and commanded his strongest soldiers to tie up Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego and throw them in. The flames were so intense that the soldiers who carried them were killed by the heat.
As Nebuchadnezzar watched, he was astonished. “Weren’t there three men we tied up and threw into the fire?” he asked. “But I see four men walking around in the fire, unbound and unharmed, and the fourth looks like a son of the gods!”
The King of heaven had sent His angel to protect them. Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego emerged from the furnace completely unharmed. Not a hair on their heads was singed, and they didn’t even smell of smoke.
Nebuchadnezzar declared, “Praise be to the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego, who sent His angel to rescue them! No other god can save in this way.”
This story reminds us that the true King is worthy of our complete loyalty. Even when the world demands that we bow to false gods, we are called to worship Him alone. It also points us to Jesus, who faced the fire of God’s wrath for our sins and emerged victorious. Because of His sacrifice, we can stand firm, knowing that the King of heaven is with us.
**Talk About It:**
*   Why did Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refuse to bow to the statue?
*   What happened when they were thrown into the fiery furnace?
*   How does this story encourage us to worship God alone?

### Story 33
**Title:** God Saves a Pagan King
**Scripture:** Daniel 4
**Key Verse:** “His dominion is an everlasting dominion, and his kingdom endures from generation to generation.” — Daniel 4:34
**Simple Summary:** God humbled King Nebuchadnezzar and showed him that He is the true King.
**Longer Story:**
Even though Israel and Judah rebelled against God, God remained faithful to His people, continuing to speak through His prophets and revealing His plans for their future. One of the most prominent prophets during the time of exile was Daniel, who was taken to Babylon after King Nebuchadnezzar conquered Jerusalem. Even in the foreign land of Babylon, Daniel remained steadfast in his faith, refusing to defile himself by eating the king’s food or bowing down to false gods. His commitment to God earned him the favor and attention of King Nebuchadnezzar.
One night, Nebuchadnezzar had a troubling dream that none of his wise men could interpret. In desperation, the king called for Daniel, who prayed to God for the interpretation. God revealed the meaning of the dream to Daniel. The king had dreamed of a giant statue made of various materials: gold, silver, bronze, iron, and clay. Daniel explained that the statue represented the kingdoms of the earth. However, a rock, not made by human hands, struck the statue and destroyed it. This rock symbolized God’s eternal kingdom, which would never be destroyed, unlike the earthly kingdoms.
Impressed by Daniel’s wisdom, Nebuchadnezzar declared, “Surely your God is the God of gods and the Lord of kings!” Yet, despite this acknowledgment, Nebuchadnezzar’s pride soon returned. He boasted of his own greatness, seeing himself as the builder of an unshakable empire. His pride was evident in his desire to be worshipped and his belief that his kingdom was invincible. Nebuchadnezzar forgot the lesson that God had just shown him—that the true King of heaven rules over all.
In his arrogance, Nebuchadnezzar declared that his kingdom was the result of his own strength and wisdom. He believed that no one could challenge his authority, and he lived as if he were invincible. But God had a different plan. God sent a warning to Nebuchadnezzar through another dream, telling him that he would lose his kingdom and live like an animal until he humbled himself before the Lord. Nebuchadnezzar did not listen to the warning, and as foretold, he spent seven years living in the wilderness, acting like an animal.
It was only after this humbling experience that Nebuchadnezzar recognized the sovereignty of God and repented. When he acknowledged God’s reign, his sanity and kingdom were restored. “Now I praise the King of heaven,” Nebuchadnezzar said, “because everything He does is right.” His story serves as a powerful reminder that God reigns over all nations and kings. Pride and self-sufficiency will always be humbled before the true King, Jesus Christ, who will one day rule forever and bring all nations under His authority.
**Talk About It:**
*   How did Daniel remain faithful to God while living in Babylon?
*   What did Nebuchadnezzar learn about God through his dreams?
*   How does Nebuchadnezzar’s story point to Jesus as the King of all nations?

### Story 34
**Title:** God Saves Daniel
**Scripture:** Daniel 6
**Key Verse:** “My God sent His angel, and he shut the mouths of the lions. They have not hurt me, because I was found innocent in his sight. Nor have I ever done any wrong before you, your Majesty.” — Daniel 6:22
**Simple Summary:** Daniel prayed to God, even when it was dangerous. God saved him from the lions, showing He is the true King who rescues His people.
**Longer Story:**
After the reign of Nebuchadnezzar, the Babylonian Empire was conquered by the Medes and Persians, and King Darius took the throne. Darius, impressed by Daniel’s wisdom, integrity, and faithfulness, made him one of three administrators overseeing the kingdom. Soon, Daniel’s exceptional qualities and abilities stood out so much that King Darius considered making him the chief ruler over the entire empire. However, this caused jealousy among the other administrators.
The jealous officials, knowing Daniel’s devotion to God, devised a plan to trap him. They manipulated King Darius into issuing a decree that for the next thirty days, no one could pray to any god or man except the king himself. Anyone who disobeyed the law would be thrown into the den of lions. This decree was intended to catch Daniel, who was known to pray to God three times a day, without fail.
Despite the decree, Daniel remained faithful to God. He continued to pray just as he always had, with his windows open toward Jerusalem, giving thanks to the King of heaven. His devotion to God could not be swayed by the king’s law, and he boldly chose to obey God rather than men.
The jealous administrators quickly caught Daniel praying and reported him to King Darius. Although Darius was greatly distressed, he realized that he could not reverse his own law, which had been sealed with his signet ring. Reluctantly, he ordered Daniel to be thrown into the lions’ den. As Daniel was led away, the king expressed his hope, saying, “May your God rescue you.”
That night, King Darius was unable to eat or sleep, tormented by worry. At dawn, he hurried to the lions’ den and cried out, “Daniel, servant of the living God, has your God been able to rescue you from the lions?” To his amazement, Daniel responded, “My God sent His angel, and He shut the mouths of the lions. They have not hurt me because I was found innocent in His sight.”
Darius was overjoyed and immediately ordered Daniel to be lifted from the den. The king then commanded that the men who had plotted against Daniel be thrown into the lions’ den. As they fell, the lions immediately overpowered them, showing God’s justice in action.

Jesus’s death and resurrection are the heart of God’s kingdom plan. Through His sacrifice, Jesus defeated sin and death, opening the way for all who believe in Him to become part of His eternal kingdom.

### Story 45
**Title:** Jesus Sends the Holy Spirit

As they watched, Jesus was taken up into heaven, and a cloud hid Him from their sight.

### Story 46
**Title:** God Raises Up Paul
**Scripture:** Acts 9
**Key Verse:** “Go, for he is a chosen instrument of mine to carry my name before the Gentiles and kings and the children of Israel.” — Acts 9:15
**Simple Summary:** Jesus appeared to Saul and changed his heart. Saul, later called Paul, became a great missionary, spreading the gospel to many people.
**Longer Story:**
The early church was rapidly growing, but it also faced significant opposition. Many of the religious leaders in Jerusalem rejected Jesus as the Messiah and sought to stop His followers from spreading His message. Among the most zealous of these opponents was a man named Saul.
Saul was a devout Pharisee, deeply committed to the Jewish religion, and he believed he was doing God’s work by persecuting Christians. He hunted down believers, arresting them and even approving of their deaths. He was present when Stephen, the first Christian martyr, was stoned to death, and he continued his mission to destroy the church by traveling from city to city, seeking to imprison or kill more followers of Jesus.
However, the King of heaven had a different plan for Saul’s life, one that would change him forever. As Saul traveled to Damascus to continue his campaign against the Christians, something extraordinary happened. Suddenly, a bright light from heaven flashed around him, and he fell to the ground. Confused and startled, Saul responded, “Who are You, Lord?”
The voice replied, “I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting. Now get up and go into the city, and you will be told what you must do.”
Blinded by the intense light, Saul was led into Damascus, where he remained in darkness for three days, fasting and praying. Meanwhile, Jesus spoke to a disciple named Ananias in a vision, telling him to go to Saul and restore his sight. Paul’s story serves as a powerful reminder that no one is beyond the reach of God’s grace. Even someone as zealous and opposed to the gospel as Saul could be transformed by the King of heaven. Saul’s conversion shows us that God can take the hardest of hearts and use them for His glory, preaching the gospel across the world.

### Story 47

Paul’s letters to the churches he founded, such as Romans, Galatians, and Ephesians, continue to teach us about the gospel and what it means to live as citizens of God’s kingdom.

### Story 48

One of the most powerful stories from Paul’s missionary journeys took place in the city of Philippi, a Roman colony in Macedonia. Paul and Silas were preaching the gospel and seeing lives changed when they encountered a slave girl who had a spirit of divination, allowing her to predict the future. Her owners used her abilities to make a profit, but when Paul cast the evil spirit out of her in the name of Jesus, they lost their source of income. Suddenly, a violent earthquake struck, shaking the prison so violently that the doors flew open, and everyone’s chains were loosed. That very night, the jailer and his family were baptized, and their lives were transformed by the power of God. In a beautiful act of hospitality and repentance, the jailer washed Paul and Silas’s wounds, and the group shared a meal together, rejoicing in their newfound faith and salvation.

Paul and Silas’s unwavering faith in the King of heaven turned a night of suffering into an opportunity to share the good news of Jesus Christ. ### Story 49

**Key Verse:** “For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek.” — Romans 1:16
**Simple Summary:** Paul preached the gospel, and many people turned from false gods to follow Jesus. The gospel changes lives.
**Longer Story:**
Paul’s missionary journeys took him to many cities across the Roman Empire, and everywhere he went, the power of the gospel transformed lives. One of the most remarkable examples of this was in the city of Ephesus, a bustling center of trade and culture. Ephesus was also known for its worship of false gods, especially Artemis, whose grand temple was considered one of the wonders of the ancient world.
When Paul arrived in Ephesus, he began teaching about Jesus and the kingdom of God. He stayed there for two years, preaching in the synagogue, teaching daily in a lecture hall, and performing miracles by the power of the Holy Spirit. Many people came to believe in Jesus, and the news of Paul’s ministry spread throughout the region.
During his time in Ephesus, Paul’s message began to challenge the deeply rooted pagan beliefs of the people. One day, some men who practiced sorcery tried to use Jesus’s name to cast out demons. They said, “In the name of the Jesus whom Paul preaches, I command you to come out.” But the evil spirit answered, “Jesus I know, and Paul I know about, but who are you?” Then the possessed man attacked them, and they fled, beaten and terrified.
When the people of Ephesus heard about this, they were filled with awe and fear. Many who practiced sorcery brought their scrolls and burned them publicly. The value of the scrolls was enormous, but the people realized that following the true King meant leaving their old ways behind. The word of the Lord spread widely, and the church in Ephesus grew.
However, not everyone welcomed Paul’s message. A silversmith named Demetrius, who made idols of Artemis, stirred up a riot. He told the craftsmen, “This man Paul says that gods made by human hands are no gods at all. If people believe him, our business will fail, and the great temple of Artemis will lose its honor.”
The city was thrown into chaos as a mob filled the streets, shouting, “Great is Artemis of the Ephesians!” Paul wanted to address the crowd, but his friends urged him to stay away for his safety. Eventually, the city clerk calmed the mob, and Paul left Ephesus, continuing his mission.
The events in Ephesus show that the gospel has the power to challenge false beliefs and transform lives. Paul’s ministry reminds us that Jesus, the true King, calls His people to turn from sin and worship Him alone. The gospel is the power of God for salvation, bringing people out of darkness and into the light of His kingdom. This story emphasizes the ongoing impact of the gospel, calling believers to boldly proclaim the message of Christ even when faced with opposition.

*   How does the story of Ephesus show the power of the gospel to change lives?

### Story 50
**Title:** The Church of Jesus Christ

**Key Verse:** “Now you are the body of Christ and individually members of it.” — 1 Corinthians 12:27
**Simple Summary:** The church is the body of Christ, made up of all who believe in Jesus. Together, we worship God, love one another, and share the gospel with the world.
**Longer Story:**
As Paul preached the gospel and planted churches across the Roman Empire, he taught believers what it meant to be part of the church, the body of Christ. The church is not a building or a specific place but the people of God—those who believe in Jesus and follow Him as King. Paul described the church as a body, with each member playing an important role. “Just as a body has many parts,” he wrote to the Corinthians, “so it is with Christ. If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? God has placed each part in the body just as He wanted it to be.” Each believer has a distinct role and gift, but all are necessary for the church to function as it should. No one part is more important than another, but together, the church can fulfill the purposes God has for it.
Paul encouraged the churches to work together in unity, using their gifts to serve one another. Some were called to teach, others to lead, and others to show mercy or generosity. “If one part suffers, every part suffers with it,” Paul wrote. “If one part is honored, every part rejoices with it.” This emphasizes the deep interconnectedness of believers. The church is not meant to be a group of isolated individuals but a community where each member supports and encourages one another.
The early church faced many challenges, including persecution, false teaching, and disagreements among believers. Paul wrote letters to address these issues, urging the churches to stand firm in the gospel and follow the example of Jesus. He knew that in order to thrive and grow, the church needed to be grounded in truth, love, and mutual care for one another.
One of Paul’s most beautiful descriptions of the church is found in his letter to the Ephesians. “Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her,” Paul wrote, “to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word.” He reminded the Ephesians that the church is the bride of Christ, deeply loved and cherished by the King of heaven. This love and sacrifice of Christ should inspire the church to love one another and to serve the world.
Paul also taught that the church’s mission is to proclaim the good news of Jesus to the world. “We are therefore Christ’s ambassadors,” he wrote, “as though God were making His appeal through us.” The church exists to glorify God and make disciples of all nations, pointing people to the true King and His kingdom.
Through Paul’s teaching, we see that the church is central to God’s plan for the world. As the body of Christ, we are called to worship the King, love one another, and share the gospel, knowing that Jesus reigns as head over His church forever.

*   What is the mission of the church, and how does it point people to Jesus?
*   How does the church reflect God’s love and plan for the world?

### Story 53

The story of the Bible ends as it began, with God dwelling with His people in perfect harmony. Through Jesus, the true King, God’s plan to redeem His creation is fulfilled, and His glory is displayed forever.
